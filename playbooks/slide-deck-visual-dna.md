> **Purpose:** The definitive reference for *how slides should look and be built* — visual hierarchy, information density, storytelling through layout, and leave-behind design principles. This is the VISUAL layer — complementing `compelling-ai-sales-pptx.md` (content strategy).
>
> **Research basis:** Andy Raskin strategic narrative framework, McKinsey/BCG consulting slide methodology, Mayer multimedia learning theory, 300+ enterprise deck analysis (Deckary), leave-behind vs. presentation deck design patterns, and validated through production decks (a card-issuer client sandbox engagement, a wealth-management client AI Agent POV).

## The Core Principle

**A slide is a visual argument, not a text container.**

Every slide must pass the **5-second test**: show it to someone for 5 seconds. If they can't tell you what the slide is about, it fails. The fix is never "add more text" — it's restructure the visual hierarchy.

---

## Two Modes: Know Which You're Building

| Mode | Purpose | Text Density | Self-Sufficiency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Presentation deck** | Live delivery support | Max 20 words/slide | Incomplete without speaker |
| **Leave-behind** | Circulates without you | Max 60 words/slide | Must tell the full story alone |

**Tavant default: Leave-behind.** Our decks get forwarded to people who weren't in the room. The champion reads it at 10pm. The CIO gets it forwarded from their direct report. It must work without a narrator.

### Leave-Behind Design Rules

1. **Headlines carry the argument.** Read only the titles in sequence — they must tell a coherent story (McKinsey "horizontal flow")
2. **Every data point has context.** A stat alone says "here's data." A stat with an annotation says "here's what it means and why it matters"
3. **Transitions are explicit.** Section dividers, visual cues, or "so what" framing slides bridge sections
4. **Each slide stands alone.** Someone seeing only one slide (forwarded in an email) must understand the point
5. **Speaker notes = extended narrative.** For slides with compressed text, use notes as the written voiceover for async readers

## The Strategic Narrative DNA (Andy Raskin Framework)

The most effective B2B sales decks follow this structure — validated across Zuora, Drift, Salesforce, and now Tavant's production decks:

### The 5 Elements

| # | Element | What It Does | Visual Expression |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Name a Big Change** | Creates urgency without mentioning your product | Dark/dramatic headline slide with provocative statement |
| 2 | **Winners and Losers** | Overcomes loss aversion | ERA framing (Before/After contrast), competitive context |
| 3 | **Tease the Promised Land** | Shows the future state (not your product) | Aspirational headline + open question |
| 4 | **Magic Gifts** | Your capabilities as tools for reaching Promised Land | Capability cards with visual structure (pillars, phases) |
| 5 | **Evidence** | Proof you can deliver the story | Stats, case metrics, production results |

### Key Insight: Client Is the Hero

- **Wrong:** "Tavant offers AI governance framework with 4 pillars..."
- **Right:** "FASTEST AI ADOPTION IN WEALTH MANAGEMENT. NOT YET GOVERNED FOR PHASE 2."

The client's situation IS the opening. Your product appears only as the means to their Promised Land.

## Visual Hierarchy Rules

### The 3-Level System

Every slide has exactly three levels of visual weight:

| Level | Purpose | Size | Weight | Color |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L1 — Hero** | What the slide IS about | 22-26pt | Bold | White (on dark) or Dark (on light) |
| **L2 — Support** | How/why the hero matters | 10-11pt | Bold | Dark text or orange accent |
| **L3 — Detail** | Evidence and context | 8.5-9.5pt | Normal | Gray (#555555 - #999999) |

**Rule:** If you can't assign every text element to L1, L2, or L3 — you have too many competing elements.

### Spacing & Density

| Metric | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Whitespace | 15-20% of slide area empty | Signals confidence, reduces cognitive load |
| Words per slide | 25-40 (leave-behind max: 60) | Beyond this, you're writing a doc, not a deck |
| Ideas per slide | ONE | "If it needs paragraphs, it needs multiple slides" |
| Card columns | 3-4 max | More creates visual noise |
| Stat callouts | 3 max per slide | More dilutes impact |
| Body text minimum | 8.5pt (projected) / 9pt (read-ahead) | Below this = unreadable at distance |

### Color Architecture

Use a **3-color system** with strict hierarchy:

| Role | Tavant | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Dark (#1A1A1A / #1F2937) | Headers, text, anchoring |
| **Accent** | Orange (#F36E26) | Headlines, labels, accent bars, CTA |
| **Signal** | Green/Red/Gray | Data (good/bad/neutral) |

**Rule:** Orange appears in exactly 3-5 places per slide — never more. If everything is orange, nothing is orange.

## Layout Patterns That Work

### Pattern 1: Zoned Slide (Horizontal Bands)

The slide is divided into 3-5 horizontal zones, each with a clear purpose. The eye scans top→bottom in a predictable rhythm.

**Best for:** Dense information slides, the "Insight" slide.

### Pattern 2: Card Grid (3-4 Columns)

Equal-width cards with consistent internal structure (title + body). Each card is a self-contained unit.

**Best for:** Capabilities, deliverables, phases, features.

**Card anatomy:**
- Accent bar (top or left, 4-6px, brand color)
- Title (bold, 10-11pt)
- Body (3-4 lines, 8.5-9pt)
- Optional: icon, metric, checkmark

### Pattern 3: Big Number + Context

One large stat dominates the visual field. Supporting text below explains what it means.

**Best for:** Stats slides, cost-of-inaction, proof points.

### Pattern 4: Before → After (Era Framing)

Two columns or horizontal sections explicitly contrasting old vs. new state.

**Best for:** Problem framing, paradigm shifts, "why now" arguments.

### Pattern 5: Numbered Sequence

01 → 02 → 03 → 04 with large bold numbers creating rhythm.

**Best for:** Process, timeline, phases, implementation steps.

## Typography System

### For pptxgenjs (LAYOUT_WIDE = 13.33" × 7.5")

| Element | Font | Size | Weight | Spacing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Category label | Arial | 8.5pt | Bold | charSpacing: 1-1.5 |
| Hero headline | Arial | 22-26pt | Bold | — |
| Section header | Arial | 8.5pt | Bold | charSpacing: 1 |
| Card title | Arial | 10-11pt | Bold | — |
| Body text | Arial | 8.5-9.5pt | Normal | — |
| Stat number | Arial | 20-36pt | Bold | — |
| Footer text | Arial | 8-8.5pt | Normal | — |
| Page number | Arial | 8pt | Normal | — |

### Title Rules

- ALL CAPS for category labels and section headers
- Title Case or Sentence case for headlines (never ALL CAPS for long headlines — harder to read)
- ALL CAPS acceptable for short provocative lines (< 8 words)

## Anti-Patterns (What Makes Decks "Amateurish")

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Walls of text | Forces reading, not scanning | One idea per slide, 3-level hierarchy |
| Centered body text | Eye hunts for line starts | Left-align body; center only headlines |
| Feature lists without context | "So what?" — reader doesn't know why to care | Every feature tied to a pain point or outcome |
| Generic stock photos | Damages credibility, signals lazy | Use diagrams, icons, or nothing |
| Everything same size/weight | No hierarchy = no guidance | L1/L2/L3 system strictly applied |
| Rainbow colors | Visual noise, no signal | 3-color system with strict roles |
| "About Us" opening | Nobody cares yet — earn attention first | Open with THEIR situation, not yours |
| Seller-centric framing | "We offer..." instead of "You face..." | Client is the hero; you're the guide |
| Dense grids/matrices | Overwhelming, not scannable | Break into multiple slides or simplify to key insight |
| No visual variety | Every slide looks the same → monotony | Alternate patterns (cards, stats, zones, sequences) |

## Quality Gate Checklist

Before any deck ships:

### Content Quality
- [ ] Headlines alone tell a coherent story (horizontal flow test)
- [ ] Client is the hero — not Tavant
- [ ] Every stat has context (what it means, why it matters)
- [ ] Competitive context creates urgency
- [ ] Ends with specific next step (not "Questions?")
- [ ] No feature-first slides — outcomes first

### Visual Quality
- [ ] Passes 5-second test (every slide)
- [ ] 3-level hierarchy applied consistently
- [ ] Max 3 colors + their tints
- [ ] 15-20% whitespace minimum
- [ ] No centered body text
- [ ] Visual variety (never 3+ identical layouts in sequence)
- [ ] All text readable at intended viewing distance

### Leave-Behind Quality
- [ ] Works without a narrator
- [ ] Each slide stands alone if forwarded
- [ ] Data points annotated with meaning
- [ ] Transitions explicit (section dividers or framing text)
- [ ] Champion can explain the deck to their boss from titles alone
